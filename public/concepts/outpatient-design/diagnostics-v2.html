Why the pin matters more than it looks

Your two ideas turn out to be the same idea. You wanted age-grouping so the list stops being unwieldy — but age-grouping is exactly what would bury the thing you said you most need to remember: "the crazy antibody that I found five years ago." A five-year-old result is precisely what a recency-collapse hides.

So the pin isn't a nice-to-have sitting next to the filter — it's the release valve that makes aggressive collapsing safe. You can hide fifteen years behind three counts because anything that matters forever is already floated to the top. Build them together or neither works.

Why this row is the whole argument

Look at the JCV antibody index: 0.31 → 0.42 → 0.88 → 1.62. That's an MS patient on natalizumab whose PML risk is climbing. In free text those four numbers live in four different visit notes across fifteen months, and nobody sees the slope — each individual value looks unremarkable in isolation. One row makes it obvious.

That's the strongest case for your instinct, and it generalizes: AChR titer in myasthenia, ESR/CRP through a giant-cell-arteritis steroid taper, CK in myopathy, AED levels in pregnancy. The clinical value isn't the value — it's the delta, and free text has no concept of a delta.

What I will not do is hardcode the thresholds. The JCV index risk cutoffs are widely cited around 0.9 and 1.5, but my research pass could not confirm them against a primary source, so the mockup shows the value rising and flags it — it does not assert a risk stratification. Same for every reference range here. Those need your sign-off or a pharmacy source before they go anywhere near a real screen.

Serial vs. one-time — and why it can't be a fixed property

The research surfaced a clean split that turns out not to be clean:

📈 Serially tracked — the table
  • AChR / MuSK antibody titer — myasthenia; serial monitoring at 3 and 6 months is recommended practice
  • JCV antibody index — MS on natalizumab; checked at baseline then roughly every 6 months
  • AED levels — phenytoin (free + total), valproate, carbamazepine, levetiracetam (essential in pregnancy)
  • ESR + CRP — GCA steroid taper. Caveat: both are suppressed by tocilizumab regardless of disease activity
  • CK — myopathy, statin myopathy
  • Vitamin D, CBC, LFTs — AED safety and bone health
  • HbA1c — diabetic neuropathy
📌 One-time — the pin
  • CSF oligoclonal bands / IgG index — once positive, essentially permanent
  • Genetic results — HD CAG repeat, SCA panel, ALS panel, NOTCH3/CADASIL. Germline; literally never changes
  • Paraneoplastic / autoimmune encephalitis panel — NMDAR, LGI1, CASPR2, anti-Hu/Yo/Ri
  • Myositis-specific antibodies — Jo-1, Mi-2, MDA5, HMGCR. CK is the serial companion, not the antibody
  • Ceruloplasmin / copper — Wilson's workup
  • HIV, RPR, celiac, ANA, SPEP/IFE — one-time screens
The design consequence — don't hardcode the classification

Several labs refuse the binary. AQP4-IgG titer tracks severity during an attack but not between them. B12/MMA is one-time at workup but re-checked after repletion. 24-hour urine copper is one-time for diagnosis but serial once you're chelating. Oligoclonal bands can actually disappear on natalizumab over years.

So: ship a sensible default per lab and let the physician override it. Any lab can be pinned, followed, both, or neither — the classification is a suggestion, not a schema. Hardcoding it as a field type per lab will be wrong often enough to be annoying, and the exceptions are exactly the interesting patients.

Where this lands you with Prachi

Worth noticing: this quietly hands Prachi some of what she wanted without breaking your free-text position. Her objection was "free texting every piece of this is going to be challenging for data mining." Tracked labs are structured name/value/unit/date rows — genuinely mineable, trendable, reportable. And they got that way because a physician chose to follow a lab for clinical reasons, not because a form demanded it.

That's a better outcome than either side argued for in the meeting. It's also a real answer to your MIDAS quality-metric ambition — "has the patient's MIDAS score gotten better over time?" — which needs exactly this shape of data.

But the codebase already answered your open question — differently than you did

I found SCALE_LOCATION_MAP in scale-definitions.ts. It already classifies all 20 scales as exam or history, and it contradicts the working answer you gave in the meeting.

You said NIHSS is exam-embedded and essentially everything else is a historical scale. The shipped code says eight scales are exam: nihss, modified_ashworth, moca, mini_cog, updrs_motor, hoehn_yahr, edss, dn4 — and twelve are history: phq9, gad7, midas, hit6, ess, abcd2, dhi, isi, cha2ds2_vasc, has_bled, odi, ndi.

So UPDRS, EDSS and MoCA are already classified as exam — the three you specifically couldn't place. Someone made that call once and shipped it. That's not proof it's right, but it means your decision is really ratify or override, not decide from scratch — and if you override it, something already in production changes behavior. Worth knowing before you pick.
