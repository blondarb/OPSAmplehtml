Foundation

What the working session settled

These aren't proposals — the room agreed them on 15 July. Everything below is built on top.

DecisionWhy
No yes/no questions Answering "was a CT done? No" writes a useless line into the note. The current Imaging/results tab is a list of yes/no prompts; that model manufactures documentation nobody wants to read.
Pills, not dropdowns A dropdown costs a click to reveal and a scan to find. Pills show the options and cost one tap. This was emphatic and applies everywhere.
Free text first Structure is opt-in. The default entry mode for an outpatient neurologist is prose — typed, pasted, or dictated. Clickable UI has to earn its place against that.
Nothing mandatory No required fields without a hard billing or quality reason. Forcing scales produces compliance behaviour, not clinical value.
History ≠ documentation This tab records what was done. It is not an ordering surface — orders live on Recommendations. A 2015 study should be visible without entering today's note.
Searchable, longitudinal Scales, imaging and labs must be findable by typing, and reviewable as a trend across visits rather than as isolated snapshots.

Tab 1

Physical exams

The design problem: a normal neuro exam is roughly forty assertions. An abnormal one is those forty, minus three. Every clickable exam ever built makes you handle all forty to express the three — which is why they always feel inefficient. The fix is to make the exception the only thing that occupies space.

The interaction, precisely

Negating a normal is not the same as deleting it. Turning off "5/5 throughout" leaves a hole — it doesn't say what the abnormal is. So one gesture does both: click the phrase, state the finding, and the prose reassembles around it.

The clinician clicks the note text, not a checkbox beside it. You're editing the document directly, with structure underneath. That's what lets free-text-first and structured capture stop being a trade-off — the output is the interface, so there's no mental translation from checkbox to prose.

Why the default template is a safety feature, not a convenience

An empty template is a blank page, and a blank page is why "author your own normal exam" has never actually happened. Shipping one means the feature works on day one for someone who never opens a settings screen.

But the important part is what it omits. A default that silently asserted reflexes would be documenting an exam nobody can perform over video. Reflexes, fundoscopy and fine sensory are left out by construction. If a clinician wants them, they add them deliberately — which is exactly the right friction.

Authoring — no settings screen

The template is authored in place: do a normal exam on a real patient, get the wording how you like it, hit Save as mine. A favourite is saved from something, not composed in a vacuum — and a template written in a settings screen, away from a patient, is written in the wrong headspace. A settings area can earn its way in later, once there are several things to put in it.

Which scales appear on this tab

Only exam-derived, outpatient-relevant ones — roughly eight, not the whole catalog. Acute and ICU scales (GCS, Hunt-Hess, FOUR, RACE, CAM-ICU, RASS, WFNS) stay out of the outpatient picker even though they exist in the catalog.

Four or five pills, not forty. Search covers the long tail without a menu.
Fitting ~40 study types without a dropdown

Pills for the common studies, typeahead search for everything else. A search box isn't a dropdown — no click to open, no list to scan; type "orb" and get MRI orbits. The pills stay short and cover most visits; the long tail stays reachable without ever rendering a menu.

The gap list this fixes is real: evoked potentials, OCT, cardiac workup, autonomic testing, DaTscan, skin biopsy, vestibular testing, neuropsych, and the MRI protocol variants (orbits, pituitary, IAC, MRV) that "MRI Brain" currently collapses together. The authority on what to include is Sevaro's own order volume, not a literature sweep — the procedures list from the working session should override anything here.

The pin and the collapse are one feature

Age-grouping is what makes a 15-year record readable. It's also exactly what would bury the five-year-old antibody result you most need to remember — a recency-collapse hides old things, and old-but-critical is the whole problem.

So the pin isn't a nice-to-have next to the filter. It's the release valve that makes aggressive collapsing safe: you can hide fifteen years behind three counts because anything that matters forever is already floated to the top. Build them together or neither works.

Pins are personal bookmarks, not shared clinical flags — unpin and repin freely; the data itself never moves and stays findable in the full record. And a pinned result doesn't enter the note unless you ask it to.

Why a table and not more prose

Look at the first row: 0.31 → 0.42 → 0.88 → 1.62. In free text those four numbers live in four different visit notes across fifteen months, and nobody sees the slope — each value looks unremarkable alone. One row makes it obvious.

That generalises to every serially-tracked lab: antibody titres, drug levels, inflammatory markers through a steroid taper, CK in myopathy. The clinical value isn't the value — it's the delta, and free text has no concept of a delta.

Reference ranges and thresholds are deliberately not asserted here and need clinical sign-off before they appear on a real screen.

One schema warning

Whether a lab is "followed over time" or "remembered once" can't be a fixed property. Several sit in both camps depending on the patient — a marker that's diagnostic once may become serial if treatment starts. Ship a sensible default per lab and let the clinician override it. Hardcoding it as a field type will be wrong often enough to annoy, and the exceptions are exactly the interesting patients.

Cross-cutting

Scales — where they live

The rule the working session converged on, which resolves most placement arguments without a vote:

The classification rule

Does producing this number require a physical maneuver at this encounter?

Examiner performs or observes it → exam tab. NIHSS, MoCA, EDSS, UPDRS-III, Hoehn & Yahr, Modified Ashworth, MRC sum score.

Patient or informant reports it → history tab. MIDAS, PHQ-9, GAD-7, ESS, ISI, DHI, ODI, NDI, ALSFRS-R, MG-ADL, modified Rankin, Barthel.

The test is what generates the data, not who writes the number down. Modified Rankin is clinician-assigned but derived entirely from a structured interview about function — nothing is examined — so it's history. Same for Barthel. That a clinician assigns the score no more makes it an exam than calculating a risk score does.

The trend matters more than the catalog size

Twenty scales with no longitudinal view is worth less than eight scales with one. The sequencing argument: build the trend against the scales that already exist, prove a 180-day outcome works end to end, and only then add more. Adding scales to a product that can't trend them just makes more data nobody can see.

Note this also means the trend component gets mounted twice — history-based scales trend on the History tab, exam-based ones on the exam tab. Build it once.

Licensing — a real constraint on which scales ship

Some widely-used instruments are commercially licensed or require user certification. A licensing review is in progress and will determine what can be included.

The useful distinction isn't licensed vs. free. It's encumbered-and-redundant — where a free instrument already covers the same ground, so dropping it costs nothing — versus encumbered-and-load-bearing, where there's no substitute of equal clinical acceptance and the choice is pay or accept a real gap. Only the first group is free money. Nothing here is shipped, so this is a cheap decision now and an expensive one later.

Proposed behaviour when a clinician searches for an unavailable scale: say plainly why it isn't offered, suggest the available alternative, and let them register a request. That converts a recurring governance debate into a demand signal — and the request counter is worth logging from day one even if the screen is post-MVP, because a year of demand data can't be backfilled.

The last row is the argument

The total says "MIDAS improved 32 → 14, treatment is working." The components say something the total structurally cannot: work and household impact both fell, and social/leisure never moved. That patient is functioning at work and still not going out — a different conversation, possibly a different intervention.

The total is the metric you report; the components are the signal you use in the room. Two consumers, same data, and only one of them is served by the number alone. Stop at two levels — below a component is a single answer at a single visit, which is just the form you already filled in.

Open

Not yet decided

QuestionDetail
Vital signs and fever chips Present in the current design; never came up in three hours of discussion. Left untouched rather than redesigned on a guess. In a tele visit with an MA assisting, it isn't obvious these should be typed at all.
Scale catalog consolidation Scale definitions currently live in more than one place and have diverged. They should resolve to a single catalog with a per-surface filter — an outpatient picker showing outpatient scales, rather than separate catalogs per app.
UPDRS-III licensing Needs a licensing check before build. Options are permission, a differently-licensed equivalent, or no Parkinson's motor scoring.
Headache scale selection MIDAS is the proposed primary. Open question is whether any payer specifically mandates an alternative for prior authorisation — that would be the deciding factor.
Split scales At least one instrument has an examined subscale and a reported subscale. A schema that assumes one location per scale can't express that.
Study list authority The diagnostic list here is literature-derived. Sevaro's own order volume should override it.
How to read this document

Every mockup is a strawman. The parts most likely to be wrong are the ones stated most confidently — particularly the claim about why clickable exams feel inefficient, which is a theory about how neurologists work rather than an established fact. Arguing with it is the intended use.
