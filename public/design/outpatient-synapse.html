Physical exams

A normal neuro exam is roughly forty assertions. An abnormal one is those forty, minus three. Every clickable exam ever built makes you handle all forty to express the three — which is why they always feel inefficient. The fix is to make the exception the only thing that occupies space.

Yes — free text starts blank, and that's a safety answer not a preference

An earlier draft pre-filled the free-text box with the normal exam. That's wrong, and the reason matters: a pre-filled normal exam is an exam you didn't perform, already written into your note. Every keystroke you don't type is an assertion you didn't make. If it's sitting there by default, the failure mode is signing it without reading it — which is exactly the junk-documentation problem that killed yes/no, just wearing better prose.

So the two pills do genuinely different things:

  • Free text — blank. Type, paste or dictate. Nothing is asserted unless you write it. ⚡ Insert my normal exam is there if you want the template as a starting point, but you have to ask for it.
  • Clickable — pre-filled, deliberately. Choosing this pill is the act of invoking your template. Then you negate the exceptions.

Both produce the same kind of prose. The difference is whether the normal exam arrives because you asked for it or because the software decided for you.

The interaction, precisely

Negating a normal is not the same as deleting it. Turning off "5/5 throughout" leaves a hole — it doesn't say what the abnormal is. So one gesture does both: click the phrase, state the finding, and the prose reassembles around it.

The clinician clicks the note text, not a checkbox beside it. You're editing the document directly, with structure underneath. That's what lets free-text-first and structured capture stop being a trade-off — the output is the interface, so there's no mental translation from checkbox to prose.

Why the default template is a safety feature, not a convenience

An empty template is a blank page, and a blank page is why "author your own normal exam" has never actually happened. Shipping one means the feature works on day one for someone who never opens a settings screen.

But the important part is what it omits. A default that silently asserted reflexes would be documenting an exam nobody can perform over video. Reflexes, fundoscopy and fine sensory are left out by construction. If a clinician wants them, they add them deliberately — which is exactly the right friction.

What a scale looks like opened — NIHSS as the worked example

Fifteen items, one row each, options laid out horizontally so scoring is one tap per row and the whole scale is one vertical scan. Running total and severity band sit in the header and move as you score. Tap a selected value again to clear it.

The organising choices worth arguing with: items are grouped by exam domain (consciousness, cranial nerves, motor, coordination, sensory, language) rather than listed 1–11 flat, because that's the order you actually examine in. Laterality is paired and labelled left/right explicitly — 5a/5b and 6a/6b are the classic place NIHSS gets mis-scored. Zero renders green and non-zero red, so an abnormal scale reads at a glance without reading numbers. UN is a first-class option on the items that allow it, not a blank.

This is the shape any exam scale would take — MoCA, EDSS, UPDRS Part III all reduce to grouped rows with a running total. Build it once, feed it a definition.

Vitals — what belongs, and what quietly doesn't

Standard outpatient set: blood pressure, heart rate, temperature, weight and height. Not mandatory. Height and weight earn their place twice — they compute BMI, and weight drives weight-based dosing, which in outpatient neurology means IVIG for CIDP, myasthenia and GBS. That's not a nice-to-have for those patients.

Glucose and oxygen saturation are in the current design and are proposed for removal. They're acute-stroke carryovers — glucose is a thrombolysis criterion, not an ambulatory vital. Nobody named them as standard outpatient vitals. They're cheap to keep if the MAs already capture them, but they're currently there by inheritance rather than intent.

Most of this lives in Epic, but the physician may not pull it — so these are quick-entry fields with an explicit pull action, not free text.

The split-scale problem — and it isn't hypothetical

Some instruments have an examined part and a reported part, so one scale lives on two tabs. The obvious worry is that this is an edge case invented by a schema. It isn't: the MDS-UPDRS is exactly this shape, and it's already in the catalog.

Parts I, II and IV are interview — non-motor experiences, daily living, motor complications. Part III is the motor exam. Today only Part III is built, which is why the split has stayed invisible. Add the rest and the scale immediately spans History and Exam.

The proposed handling: whichever tab you start on, the other shows what's already captured and offers to continue. The parts carry their own scores; the total assembles across both. A schema with one location per scale can't express that — it needs a location per part.

Fitting ~40 study types without a dropdown

Pills for the common studies, typeahead search for everything else. A search box isn't a dropdown — no click to open, no list to scan; type "orb" and get MRI orbits. The pills stay short and cover most visits; the long tail stays reachable without ever rendering a menu.

The authority on what to include is Sevaro's own order volume, not a literature sweep.

Pills are anatomy. "Other" is everything else — and that split matters

An earlier draft had "MRI brain — MS protocol" as a pill. That was a modelling error: it mixed a protocol into a list of anatomy. Once you do that the list has no natural end — MS protocol, seizure protocol, epilepsy thin-slice, tumour protocol, and every site names them differently.

So: pills are the standard anatomical studies — brain, orbits, pituitary, IAC, C-spine, T-spine, L-spine, MRA, MRV. Every modality also ends in "Other", which opens a text field. MS protocol goes there. So does seizure protocol, and so does CT femur. The pill list stays short and stable; the tail stays typeable and never needs a product decision to accommodate.

That also means the pill list can be opinionated and small without being wrong — "Other" is the escape hatch that lets it be short.

Cardiac lives under testing, not in its own section

Cardiac studies are one pill under Diagnostic testing & procedures, expanding to TTE, TEE, Holter, event monitor, loop recorder and bubble study — rather than a top-level section of their own. They're ordered like any other test; they don't warrant separate real estate.

Labelled "Cardiac studies", not "cardiac imaging" — Holter, event monitors and loop recorders are rhythm monitoring, not imaging, so the imaging label would have been wrong for half its own subtypes. Small thing, but schema labels outlive the conversation that set them.

"Why do labs show up in the prior record?" — you spotted a real ambiguity

You read those rows as the tagged ones. They weren't — they were every lab, which is exactly why it was confusing. Two things were competing to be "the labs".

The resolution: they answer different questions. The prior record is chronological — what happened, and when — and it holds everything: imaging, testing, cardiac, labs. The labs table is longitudinal — how did this one value move. A tracked lab legitimately appears in both, so it now carries a 📈 marker in the record to say "this one also has a trend." Nothing is duplicated in meaning; one is a timeline, the other is a series.

Placement: the very bottom. Entry sections first — imaging, testing, labs — then the record last. You come to this tab to record what happened at this visit; the fifteen-year archive is reference, not workflow. Putting it between entry sections would interrupt the thing you came to do. And the pins already float the must-not-forget items to the top, which is what makes it safe to bury the archive at the bottom.

The pin and the collapse are one feature

Age-grouping is what makes a 15-year record readable. It's also exactly what would bury the five-year-old antibody result you most need to remember — a recency-collapse hides old things, and old-but-critical is the whole problem.

So the pin isn't a nice-to-have next to the filter. It's the release valve that makes aggressive collapsing safe: you can hide fifteen years behind three counts because anything that matters forever is already floated to the top.

Pins are personal bookmarks, not shared clinical flags — unpin and repin freely; the data itself never moves and stays findable in the full record. A pinned result doesn't enter the note unless you ask it to.

Why a table and not more prose

Look at the first row: 0.31 → 0.42 → 0.88 → 1.62. In free text those four numbers live in four different visit notes across fifteen months, and nobody sees the slope — each value looks unremarkable alone. One row makes it obvious.

That generalises to every serially-tracked lab: antibody titres, drug levels, inflammatory markers through a steroid taper, CK in myopathy. The clinical value isn't the value — it's the delta, and free text has no concept of a delta.

Reference ranges and thresholds are deliberately not asserted here and need clinical sign-off before they appear on a real screen.

One schema warning

Whether a lab is "followed over time" or "remembered once" can't be a fixed property. Several sit in both camps depending on the patient — a marker that's diagnostic once may become serial if treatment starts. Ship a sensible default per lab and let the clinician override it. Hardcoding it as a field type will be wrong often enough to annoy, and the exceptions are exactly the interesting patients.

Cross-cutting

Scales — where they live

The classification rule

Does producing this number require a physical maneuver at this encounter?

Examiner performs or observes it → exam tab. NIHSS, MoCA, EDSS, UPDRS-III, Hoehn & Yahr, Modified Ashworth, MRC sum score.

Patient or informant reports it → history tab. MIDAS, PHQ-9, GAD-7, ESS, ISI, DHI, ODI, NDI, ALSFRS-R, MG-ADL, modified Rankin, Barthel.

The test is what generates the data, not who writes the number down. Modified Rankin is clinician-assigned but derived entirely from a structured interview about function — nothing is examined — so it's history. Same for Barthel. That a clinician assigns the score no more makes it an exam than calculating a risk score does.

Why the drill-down earns its build

A total says "MIDAS improved 32 → 14, treatment is working." The components say something the total structurally cannot — and in this patient, work and household impact both fell while social/leisure never moved. That patient is functioning at work and still not going out. Different conversation, possibly different intervention.

The total is the metric you report; the components are the signal you use in the room. Two consumers, same data, and only one is served by the number alone. Stop at two levels — below a component is a single answer at a single visit, which is just the form you already filled in.

The trend matters more than the catalog size

Twenty scales with no longitudinal view is worth less than eight scales with one. Build the trend against the scales that already exist, prove a 180-day outcome works end to end, and only then add more. Adding scales to a product that can't trend them just makes more data nobody can see.

Note this also means the trend component gets mounted twice — history-based scales trend on History, exam-based ones on the exam tab. Build it once.

Licensing — a real constraint on which scales ship

Some widely-used instruments are commercially licensed or require user certification. A licensing review is in progress and will determine what can be included.

The useful distinction isn't licensed vs. free. It's encumbered-and-redundant — where a free instrument already covers the same ground, so dropping it costs nothing — versus encumbered-and-load-bearing, where there's no substitute of equal clinical acceptance and the choice is pay or accept a real gap. Only the first group is free money. Nothing here is shipped, so this is a cheap decision now and an expensive one later.

Proposed behaviour when a clinician searches for an unavailable scale: say plainly why it isn't offered, suggest the available alternative, and let them register a request. That converts a recurring governance debate into a demand signal — and the request counter is worth logging from day one even if the screen is post-MVP, because a year of demand data can't be backfilled.

How to read this document

Every mockup is a strawman. The parts most likely to be wrong are the ones stated most confidently — particularly the claim about why clickable exams feel inefficient, which is a theory about how neurologists work rather than an established fact. Arguing with it is the intended use.
